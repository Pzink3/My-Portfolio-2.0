Epilepsy (from the Ancient Greek verb ἐπιλαμβάνειν meaning "to seize, possess, or afflict")[1] is a group of long-term neurological disorders characterized by epileptic seizures.[2] These seizures are episodes that can vary from brief and nearly undetectable to long periods of vigorous shaking.[3] In epilepsy, seizures tend to recur, and have no immediate underlying cause[2] while seizures that occur due to a specific cause are not deemed to represent epilepsy.[4]

In most cases the cause is unknown, although some people develop epilepsy as the result of brain injury, stroke, brain tumor, and drug and alcohol misuse, among others. Epileptic seizures are the result of excessive and abnormal cortical nerve cell activity in the brain.[4] The diagnosis typically involves ruling out other conditions that might cause similar symptoms (such as syncope) as well as figuring out whether any immediate causes are present. Epilepsy can often be confirmed with an electroencephalogram (EEG).

Seizures are controllable with medication in about 70% of cases.[5] In those whose seizures do not respond to medication, surgery, neurostimulation or dietary changes may be considered. Not all cases of epilepsy are lifelong, and a substantial number of people improve to the point that medication is no longer needed.

About 1% of people worldwide (65 million) have epilepsy,[6] and nearly 80% of cases occur in developing countries.[3] Epilepsy becomes more common as people age.[7][8] In the developed world, onset of new cases occurs most frequently in infants and the elderly;[9] in the developing world this is in older children and young adults,[10] due to differences in the frequency of the underlying causes. About 5–10% of all people will have an unprovoked seizure by the age of 80,[11] and the chance of experiencing a second seizure is between 40 and 50%.[12] In many areas of the world those with epilepsy either have their ability to drive restricted or disallowed,[13] but most are able to return to driving after a period of time without seizures.

Signs and symptoms[edit]

A video of a seizure
Someone who has bitten the tip of their tongue while having a seizure

Epilepsy is characterized by a long-term risk of recurrent seizures.[14] These seizures may present in several ways depending on the part of the brain involved and the person's age.[14][15]

Seizures[edit]

The most common type (60%) of seizures are convulsive.[15] Of these, two-thirds begin as focal seizures (which may then become generalized) while one-third begin as generalized seizures.[15] The remaining 40% of seizures are non-convulsive. An example of this type is the absence seizure, which presents as a decreased level of consciousness and usually lasts about 10 seconds.[16][17]

Focal seizures are often preceded by certain experiences, known as an aura.[18] These may include sensory (visual, hearing or smell), psychic, autonomic, or motor phenomena.[16] Jerking activity may start in a specific muscle group and spread to surrounding muscle groups in which case it is known as a Jacksonian march.[19] Automatisms may occur; these are non-consciously generated activities and mostly simple repetitive movements like smacking of the lips or more complex activities such as attempts to pick something up.[19]

There are six main types of generalized seizures: tonic-clonic, tonic, clonic, myoclonic, absence, and atonic seizures.[20] They all involve loss of consciousness and typically happen without warning.

Tonic-clonic seizures present with a contraction of the limbs followed by their extension along with arching of the back which lasts 10–30 seconds (the tonic phase). A cry may be heard due to contraction of the chest muscles. This is then followed by a shaking of the limbs in unison (clonic phase). Tonic seizures produce constant contractions of the muscles. A person often turns blue as breathing is stopped. In clonic seizures there is shaking of the limbs in unison. After the shaking has stopped it may take 10–30 minutes for the person to return to normal; this period is called the "postictal phase". Loss of bowel or bladder control may occur during a seizure.[3] The tongue may be bitten at either the tip or on the sides during a seizure.[21] In tonic-clonic seizure, bites to the sides are more common.[21] Tongue bites are also relatively common in psychogenic non-epileptic seizures.[21]

Myoclonic seizures involve spasms of muscles in either a few areas or all over.[22] Absence seizures can be subtle with only a slight turn of the head or eye blinking.[16] The person does not fall over and returns to normal right after it ends.[16] Atonic seizures involve the loss of muscle activity for greater than one second.[19] This typically occurs on both sides of the body.[19]

About 6% of those with epilepsy have seizures that are often triggered by specific events and are known as reflex seizures.[23] Those with reflex epilepsy have seizures that are only triggered by specific stimuli.[24] Common triggers include flashing lights and sudden noises.[23] In certain types of epilepsy, seizures happen more often during sleep,[25] and in other types they occur almost only when sleeping.[26]

Postictal[edit]

After the active portion of a seizure, there is typically a period of confusion referred to as the postictal period before a normal level of consciousness returns.[18] This usually lasts 3 to 15 minutes[27] but may last for hours.[28] Other common symptoms include feeling tired, headache, difficulty speaking, and abnormal behavior.[28] Psychosis after a seizure is relatively common, occurring in 6–10% of people.[29] Often people do not remember what happened during this time.[28] Localized weakness, known as Todd's paralysis, may also occur after a focal seizure. When it occurs it typically lasts for seconds to minutes but may rarely last for a day or two.[30]

Psychosocial[edit]

Epilepsy can have adverse effects on social and psychological well-being.[15] These effects may include social isolation, stigmatization, or disability.[15] They may result in lower educational achievement and worse employment outcomes.[15] Learning difficulties are common in those with the condition, and especially among children with epilepsy.[15] The stigma of epilepsy can also affect the families of those with the disease.[3]

Certain disorders occur more often in people with epilepsy, depending partly on the epilepsy syndrome present. These include depression, anxiety disorders, and migraines.[31] Attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder affects three to five times more children with epilepsy than children in the general population.[32] ADHD and epilepsy have significant consequences on a child's behavioral, learning, and social development.[33] Epilepsy is also more common in those with autism.[34]

Causes[edit]

See also: Causes of seizures

Epilepsy can have both genetic and acquired causes, with interaction of these factors in many cases.[35] Established acquired causes include serious brain trauma, stroke, tumours and problems in the brain as a result of a previous infective.[35] In about 60% of cases the cause is unknown.[3][15] Epilepsies caused by genetic, congenital, or developmental conditions are more common among younger people, while brain tumors and strokes are more likely in older people.[15]

Seizures may also occur as a consequence of other health problems;[20] if they occur right around a specific cause, such as a stroke, head injury, toxic ingestion or metabolic problem, they are known as acute symptomatic seizures and are in the broader classification of seizure-related disorders rather than epilepsy itself.[6][36]

Genetics[edit]

Genetics is believed to be involved in the majority of cases, either directly or indirectly.[37] Some epilepsies are due to a single gene defect (1–2%); most are due to the interaction of multiple genes and environmental factors.[37] Each of the single gene defects is rare, with more than 200 in all described.[38] Most genes involved affect ion channels, either directly or indirectly.[35] These include genes for ion channels themsleves, enzymes, GABA, and G protein-coupled receptors.[22]

In identical twins, if one is affected there is a 50–60% chance that the other will also be affected.[37] In non-identical twins the risk is 15%.[37] These risks are greater in those with generalized rather than focal seizures.[37] If both twins are affected, most of the time they have the same epileptic syndrome (70–90%).[37] Other close relatives of a person with epilepsy have a risk five times that of the general population.[39] Between 1 and 10% of those with Down syndrome and 90% of those with Angelman syndrome have epilepsy.[39]

Syndromes[edit]

See also: List of epilepsy syndromes

There are a number of epilepsy syndromes which are typically grouped by age of onset into neonatal period, childhood, adulthood, and those with no strong age relationship.[20] Additionally there are groups with specific constellations of symptoms, those due to specific metabolic or structural causes, and those of unknown cause.[20] The ability to classify a case of epilepsy into a specific syndrome occurs more often with children.[36] Some types include benign rolandic epilepsy (2.8 per 100,000), childhood absence epilepsy (0.8 per 100,000) and juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (0.7 per 100,000).[36] Febrile seizures and benign neonatal seizures are not forms of epilepsy.[20]

Acquired[edit]

Epilepsy may occur as a result of a number of other conditions including tumors, strokes, head trauma, previous infections of the central nervous system, genetic abnormalities, and as a result of brain damage around the time of birth.[3][20] Of those with brain tumors, almost 30% have epilepsy, making them the cause of about 4% of cases.[39] The risk is greatest for tumors in the temporal lobe and those that grow slowly.[39] Other mass lesions such as cerebral cavernous malformations and artiovenous malformations have risks as high as 40–60%.[39] Of those who have had a stroke, 2–4% develop epilepsy.[39] In the United Kingdom strokes account for 15% of cases[15] and it is believed to be the cause in 30% of the elderly.[39] Between 6 and 20% of epilepsy is believed to be due to head trauma.[39] Mild brain injury increases the risk about two-fold while severe brain injury increases the risk seven-fold.[39] In those who have experienced a high powered gunshot wound to the head, the risk is about 50%.[39]

The risk of epilepsy following meningitis is less than 10%; that disease more commonly causes seizures during the infection itself.[39] In herpes simplex encephalitis the risk of a seizure is around 50%[39] with a high risk of epilepsy following (up to 25%).[40][41] Infection with the pork tapeworm, which can result in neurocysticercosis, is the cause of up to half of epilepsy cases in areas of the world where the parasite is common.[39] Epilepsy may also occur after other brain infections such as cerebral malaria, toxoplasmosis, and toxocariasis.[39] Chronic alcohol use increases the risk of epilepsy: those who drink six units of alcohol per day have a two and a half fold increase in risk.[39] Other risks include Alzheimer's disease, multiple sclerosis, tuberous sclerosis, and autoimmune encephalitis.[39] Getting vaccinated does not increase the risk of epilepsy.[39] Malnutrition is a risk factor seen mostly in the developing world, although it is unclear however if it is a direct cause or an association.[10]

Mechanism[edit]

Normally brain electrical activity is non-synchronous.[16] In epileptic seizures, due to structural or functional problems within the brain,[3] a group of neurons begin firing in an abnormal, excessive,[15] and synchronized manner.[16] This results in a wave of depolarization known as a paroxysmal depolarizing shift.[42]

Normally, after an excitatory neuron fires it becomes more resistant to firing for a period of time.[16] This is due in part to the effect of inhibitory neurons, electrical changes within the excitatory neuron, and the negative effects of adenosine.[16] In epilepsy the resistance of excitatory neurons to fire during this period is decreased.[16] This may occur due to changes in ion channels or inhibitory neurons not functioning properly.[16] This then results in a specific area from which seizures may develop, known as a "seizure focus".[16] Another mechanism of epilepsy may be the up-regulation of excitatory circuits or down-regulation of inhibitory circuits following an injury to the brain.[16][43] These secondary epilepsies occur through processes known as epileptogenesis.[16][43] Failure of the blood–brain barrier may also be a causal mechanism as it would allow substances in the blood to enter the brain.[44]

Focal seizures begin in one hemisphere of the brain while generalized seizures begin in both hemispheres.[20] Some types of seizures may change brain structure, while others appear to have little effect.[45] Gliosis, neuronal loss, and atrophy of specific areas of the brain are linked to epilepsy but it is unclear if epilepsy causes these changes or if these changes result in epilepsy.[45]

Diagnosis[edit]

An EEG can aid in locating the focus of the epileptic seizure.

The diagnosis of epilepsy is typically made based on the description of the seizure and the underlying cause.[15] An electroencephalogram and neuroimaging are also usually part of the workup.[15] While figuring out a specific epileptic syndrome is often attempted, it is not always possible.[15] Video and EEG monitoring may be useful in difficult cases.[46]

Definition[edit]

Epilepsy is a disease of the brain defined by any of the following conditions:[47]

  1. At least two unprovoked (or reflex) seizures occurring greater than 24 hours apart
  2. One unprovoked (or reflex) seizure and a probability of further seizures similar to the general recurrence risk (at least 60%) after two unprovoked seizures, occurring over the next 10 years
  3. Diagnosis of an epilepsy syndrome

Furthermore epilepsy is considered to be resolved for individuals who had an age-dependent epilepsy syndrome but are now past the that age or those who have remained seizure-free for the last 10 years, with no seizure medicines for the last 5 years.[47]

This 2014 definition of the International League Against Epilepsy[47] is a refinement of their 2005 definition which was "a disorder of the brain characterized by an enduring predisposition to generate epileptic seizures and by the neurobiologic, cognitive, psychological, and social consequences of this condition. The definition of epilepsy requires the occurrence of at least one epileptic seizure."[48][49]

It is therefore possible to outgrow epilepsy or to undergo treatment that causes the epilepsy to be resolved. Resolution of epilepsy unfortunately does not guarantee that it will not return. In the definition, epilepsy is now called a disease, rather than a disorder. This was a decision of the executive committee of the ILAE, taken because the word "disorder," while perhaps having less stigma than does "disease," also does not express the degree of seriousness that epilepsy deserves.[47]

The definition is practical in nature and is designed for clinical use. Researchers, statistically-minded epidemiologists and other specialized groups may choose to use the older definition or a definition of their own devising. The ILAE considers doing so is perfectly allowable, so long as it is clear what definition is being used.[47]

Classification[edit]

In contrast to the classification of seizures which focuses on what happens during a seizure, the classification of epilepsies focuses on the underlying causes. When a person is admitted to hospital after an epileptic seizure the diagnostic workup results preferably in the seizure itself being classified (e.g. tonic-clonic) and in the underlying disease being identified (e.g. hippocampal sclerosis).[46] The name of the diagnosis finally made depends on the available diagnostic results and the applied definitions and classifications (of seizures and epilepsies) and its respective terminology.

The International League Against Epilepsy (ILAE) provided a classification of the epilepsies and epileptic syndromes in 1989 as follows:[50]

  1. Localization-related epilepsies and syndromes
    1. Idiopathic (e.g. benign childhood epilepsy with centrotemporal spikes)
    2. Symptomatic/cryptogenic (e.g. temporal lobe epilepsy)
  2. Generalized
    1. Idiopathic (e.g. childhood absence epilepsy)
    2. Cryptogenic or symptomatic (e.g. Lennox-Gastaut syndrome)
    3. Symptomatic (e.g. early infantile epileptic encephalopathy with suppression burst)
  3. Epilepsies and syndromes undetermined whether focal or generalized
    1. With both generalized and focal seizures (e.g. epilepsy with continuous spike-waves during slow wave sleep)
  4. Special syndromes (with situation-related seizures)[50]

This classification was widely accepted but has also been criticized mainly because the underlying causes of epilepsy (which are a major determinant of clinical course and prognosis) were not covered in detail.[51] In 2010 the ILAE Commission for Classification of the Epilepsies addressed this issue and divided epilepsies into three categories (genetic, structural/metabolic, unknown cause)[52] that were refined in their 2011 recommendation into four categories and a number of subcategories reflecting recent technologic and scientific advances.[53]

  1. Idiopathic (predominately genetic or presumed genetic origin)
    1. Pure epilepsies due to single gene disorders
    2. Pure epilepsies with complex inheritance
  2. Symptomatic (associated with gross anatomic or pathologic abnormalities)
    1. Predominately genetic or developmental causation
      1. Childhood epilepsy syndromes
      2. Progressive myoclonic epilepsies
      3. Neurocutaneous syndromes
      4. Other neurologic single gene disorders
      5. Disorders of chromosome function
      6. Developmental anomalies of cerebral structure
    2. Predominately acquired causation
      1. Hippocampal sclerosis
      2. Perinatal and infantile causes
      3. Cerebral trauma, tumor or infection
      4. Cerebrovascular disorders
      5. Cerebral immunologic disorders
      6. Degenerative and other neurologic conditions
  3. Provoked (a specific systemic or environmental factor is the predominant cause of the seizures)
    1. Provoking factors
    2. Reflex epilepsies
  4. Cryptogenic (presumed symptomatic nature in which the cause has not been identified)[53]

Tests[edit]

An electroencephalogram (EEG) can assist in showing brain activity suggestive of an increased risk of seizures. It is only recommended for those who are likely to have had an epileptic seizure on the basis of symptoms. In the diagnosis of epilepsy, electroencephalography may help distinguish the type of seizure or syndrome present. In children it is typically only needed after a second seizure. It cannot be used to rule out the diagnosis, and may be falsely positive in those without the disease. In certain situations it may be useful to perform the EEG while the affected individual is sleeping or sleep deprived.[46]

Diagnostic imaging by CT scan and MRI is recommended after a first non-febrile seizure to detect structural problems in and around the brain.[46] MRI is generally a better imaging test except when bleeding is suspected, for which CT is more sensitive and more easily available.[11] If someone attends the emergency room with a seizure but returns to normal quickly, imaging tests may be done at a later point.[11] If a person has a previous diagnosis of epilepsy with previous imaging, repeating the imaging is usually not needed even if there are subsequent seizures.[46]

For adults, the testing of electrolyte, blood glucose and calcium levels is important to rule out problems with these as causes.[46] An electrocardiogram can rule out problems with the rhythm of the heart.[46] A lumbar puncture may be useful to diagnose a central nervous system infection but is not routinely needed.[11] In children additional tests may be required such as urine biochemistry and blood testing looking for metabolic disorders.[46][54]

A high blood prolactin level within the first 20 minutes following a seizure may be useful to confirm an epileptic seizure as opposed to psychogenic non-epileptic seizure.[55][56] Serum prolactin level is less useful for detecting partial seizures.[57] If it is normal an epileptic seizure is still possible[56] and a serum prolactin does not separate epileptic seizures from syncope.[58] It is not recommended as a routine part of the diagnosis of epilepsy.[46]

Differential diagnosis[edit]

Diagnosis of epilepsy can be difficult. A number of other conditions may present very similar signs and symptoms to seizures, including syncope, hyperventilation, migraines, narcolepsy, panic attacks and psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES).[59][60] In particular a syncope can be accompanied by a short episode of convulsions.[61] Nocturnal frontal lobe epilepsy, often misdiagnosed as nightmares, was considered to be a parasomnia but later identified to be epileptic.[62] Attacks of the movment disorder paroxysmal dyskinesia may be taken for epileptic seizures.[63] The cause of a drop attack can be, among many others, an atonic seizure.[60]

Children may have behaviors that are easily mistaken for epileptic seizures but are not. These include breath-holding spells, bed wetting, night terrors, tics and shudder attacks.[60] Gastroesophageal reflux may cause arching of the back and twisting of the head to the side in infants, which may be mistaken for tonic-clonic seizures.[60]

Misdiagnosis is frequent (occurring in about 5 to 30% of cases).[15] Different studies showed that in many cases seizure-like attacks in apparent treatment-resistant epilepsy have a cardiovascular cause.[61][64] Approximately 20% of the people seen at epilepsy clinics have PNES[11] and of those who have PNES about 10% also have epilepsy;[65] separating the two based on the seizure episode alone without further testing is often difficult.[65]

Prevention[edit]

While many cases are not preventable, efforts to reduce head injuries, provide good care around the time of birth, and reduce environmental parasites such as the pork tapeworm may be effective.[3] Efforts in one part of Central America to decrease rates of pork tapeworm resulted in a 50% decrease in new cases of epilepsy.[10]

Management[edit]

Epilepsy is usually treated with daily medication once a second seizure has occurred,[15][46] but for those at high risk, medication may be started after the first seizure.[46] In some cases, a special diet, the implantation of a neurostimulator, or neurosurgery may be required.

First aid[edit]

Rolling a person with an active tonic-clonic seizure onto their side and into the recovery position helps prevent fluids from getting into the lungs.[66] Putting fingers, a bite block or tongue depressor in the mouth is not recommended as it might make the person vomit or result in the rescuer being bitten.[18][66] Efforts should be taken to prevent further self-injury.[18] Spinal precautions are generally not needed.[66]

If a seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes or if there are more than two seizures in an hour without a return to normal between them it is considered a medical emergency known as status epilepticus.[46][67] This may require medical help to keep the airway open and protected;[46] a nasopharyngeal airway may be useful for this.[66] At home the recommended initial medication for seizure of a long duration is midazolam placed in the mouth.[68] Diazepam may also be used rectally.[68] In hospital, intravenous lorazepam is preferred.[46] If two doses of benzodiazepines are not effective, other medications such as phenytoin are recommended.[46] Convulsive status epilepticus that does not respond to initial treatment typically requires admission to the intensive care unit and treatment with stronger agents such as thiopentone or propofol.[46]

Medications[edit]

Anticonvulsants

The mainstay treatment of epilepsy is anticonvulsant medications, possibly for the person's entire life.[15] The choice of anticonvulsant is based on seizure type, epilepsy syndrome, other medications used, other health problems, and the person's age and lifestyle.[68] A single medication is recommended initially;[69] if this is not effective, switching to a single other medication is recommended.[46] Two medications at once is only recommended if a single medication does not work.[46] In about half, the first agent is effective; a second single agent helps in about 13% and a third or two agents at the same time may help an additional 4%.[70] About 30% of people continue to have seizures despite anticonvulsant treatment.[5]

There are a number of medications available. Phenytoin, carbamazepine and valproate appear to be equally effective in both focal and generalized seizures.[71][72] Controlled release carbamazepine appears to work as well as immediate release carbamazepine, and may have fewer side effects.[73] In the United Kingdom, carbamazepine or lamotrigine are recommended as first-line treatment for focal seizures, with levetiracetam and valproate as second-line due to issues of cost and side effects.[46] Valproate is recommended first-line for generalized seizures with lamotrigine being second-line.[46] In those with absence seizures, ethosuximide or valproate are recommended; valproate is particularly effective in myoclonic seizures and tonic or atonic seizures.[46] If seizures are well-controlled on a particular treatment, it is not usually necessary to routinely check the medication levels in the blood.[46]

The least expensive anticonvulsant is phenobarbital at around $5 USD a year.[10] The World Health Organization gives it a first-line recommendation in the developing world and it is commonly used there.[74][75] Access however may be difficult as some countries label it as a controlled drug.[10]

Adverse effects from medications are reported in 10 to 90% of people, depending on how and from whom the data is collected.[76] Most adverse effects are dose-related and mild.[76] Some examples include mood changes, sleepiness, or an unsteadiness in gait.[76] Some medications have side effects that are not related to dose such as rashes, liver toxicity, or suppression of the bone marrow.[76] Up to a quarter of people stop treatment due to adverse effects.[76] Some medications are associated with birth defects when used in pregnancy.[46] Valproate is of particular concern, especially during the first trimester.[77] Despite this, treatment is often continued once effective, because the risk of untreated epilepsy is believed to be greater than the risk of the medications.[77]

Slowly stopping medications may be reasonable in some people who do not have a seizure for two to four years; however, around a third of people have a recurrence, most often during the first six months.[46][78] Stopping is possible in about 70% of children and 60% of adults.[3]

Surgery[edit]

Epilepsy surgery may be an option for people with focal seizures that remain a problem despite other treatments.[79] These other treatments include at least a trial of two or three medications.[80] The goal of surgery is total control of seizures[81] and this may be achieved in 60–70% of cases.[80] Common procedures include cutting out the hippocampus via an anterior temporal lobe resection, removal of tumors, and removing parts of the neocortex.[80] Some procedures such as a corpus callosotomy are attempted in an effort to decrease the number of seizures rather than cure the condition.[80] Following surgery, medications may be slowly withdrawn in many cases.[80]

Neurostimulation may be another option in those who are not candidates for surgery.[46] Three types have been shown to be effective in those who do not respond to medications: vagus nerve stimulation, anterior thalamic stimulation, and closed-loop responsive stimulation.[82]

Other[edit]

A ketogenic diet (high-fat, low-carbohydrate, adequate-protein) appears to decrease the number of seizures by half in about 30–40% of children.[83] About 10% manage to stay on the diet for a few years, 30% had constipation, and other adverse effects were common.[83] Less radical diets were easier to tolerate and may be effective.[83] It is unclear why this diet works.[84] Exercise has been proposed as possibly useful for preventing seizures[85] with some data to support this claim.[86]

Avoidance therapy consists of minimizing or eliminating triggers. For example, in those who are sensitive to light, using a small television, avoiding video-games or wearing dark glasses may be useful.[87] Some claim that seizure response dogs, a form of service dog, can predict seizures. Evidence for this, however, is poor.[88] Operant-based biofeedback based on the EEG waves has some support in those who do not respond to medications.[89] Psychological methods should not, however, be used to replace medications.[46]

Alternative medicine[edit]

Alternative medicine, including acupuncture,[90] psychological interventions,[91] routine vitamins,[92] and yoga,[93] have no reliable evidence to support their use in epilepsy. The use of cannabis is not supported by the evidence.[94] Melatonin is insufficiently supported by evidence.[95]

Epilepsy cannot usually be cured, but medication can control seizures effectively in about 70% of cases.[5] Of those with generalized seizure more than 80% can be well controlled with medications while this is true in only 50% of people with focal seizures.[82] One predictor of long-term outcome is the number of seizures that occur in the first six months.[15] Other factors increasing the risk of a poor outcome include little response to the initial treatment, generalized seizures, a family history of epilepsy, psychiatric problems, and waves on the EEG representing generalized epileptiform activity.[96] In the developing world 75% of people are either untreated or not appropriately treated.[3] In Africa 90% do not get treatment.[3] This is partly related to appropriate medications not being available or being too expensive.[3]

Mortality[edit]

People with epilepsy are at an increased risk of death.[97] This increase is between 1.6 and 4.1 fold greater than that of the general population[98] and is often related to: the underlying cause of the seizures, status epilepticus, suicide, trauma, and sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP).[97] Death from status epilepticus is primarily due to an underlying problem rather than missing doses of medications.[97] The risk of suicide is increased between two and six times in those with epilepsy.[99][100] The cause of this is unclear.[99] SUDEP appears to be partly related to the frequency of generalized tonic-clonic seizures[101] and accounts for about 15% of epilepsy related deaths.[96] It is unclear how to decrease its risk.[101] The greatest increase in mortality from epilepsy is among the elderly.[98] Those with epilepsy due to an unknown cause have little increased risk.[98] In the United Kingdom it is estimated that 40–60% of deaths are possibly preventable.[15] In the developing world many deaths are due to untreated epilepsy leading to falls or status epilepticus.[10]

Epidemiology[edit]

Epilepsy is one of the most common serious neurological disorders[102] affecting about 65 million people globally.[6] It affects 1% of the population by age 20 and 3% of the population by age 75.[8] It is more common in males than females with the overall difference being small.[10][36] Most of those with the disease (80%) are in the developing world.[3]

The number of people who currently have active epilepsy is in the range 5–10 per 1,000, with active epilepsy defined as someone with epilepsy who has had a least one seizure in the last five years.[36][103] Epilepsy begins each year in 40–70 per 100,000 in developed countries and 80–140 per 100,000 in developing countries.[3] Poverty is a risk and includes both being from a poor country and being poor relative to others within one's country.[10] In the developed world epilepsy most commonly starts either in the young or in the old.[10] In the developing world its onset is more common in older children and young adults due to the higher rates of trauma and infectious diseases.[10] In developed countries the number of cases a year has decreased in children and increased among the elderly between the 1970s and 2003.[103] This has been attributed partly to better survival following strokes in the elderly.[36]

History[edit]

The oldest medical records show that epilepsy has been affecting people since the beginning of recorded history.[104] Throughout ancient history, the disorder was thought to be a spiritual condition.[104] The world's oldest description of an epileptic seizure comes from a text in Akkadian (a language used in ancient Mesopotamia) and was written around 2000 BC.[1] The person described in the text was diagnosed as being under the influence of a Moon god, and underwent an exorcism.[1] Epileptic seizures are listed in the Code of Hammurabi (c. 1790 BC) as reason for which a purchased slave may be returned for a refund,[1] and the Edwin Smith Papyrus (c. 1700 BC) describes cases of individuals with epileptic convulsions.[1]

The oldest known detailed record of the disorder itself is in the Sakikkū, a Babylonian cuneiform medical text from 1067–1046 BC.[104] This text gives signs and symptoms, details treatment and likely outcomes,[1] and describes many features of the different seizure types.[104] As the Babylonians had no biomedical understanding of the nature of disease, they attributed the seizures to possession by evil spirits and called for treating the condition through spiritual means.[104] Around 900 BC, Punarvasu Ātreya described epilepsy as loss of consciousness;[105] this definition was carried forward into the Ayurvedic text of Charaka Samhita (about 400 BC).[106]

Hippocrates, 17th century engraving by Peter Paul Rubens of an antique bust.

The ancient Greeks had contradictory views of the disease. They thought of epilepsy as a form of spiritual possession, but also associated the condition with genius and the divine. One of the names they gave to it was the sacred disease.[1][107] Epilepsy appears within Greek mythology: it is associated with the Moon goddesses Selene and Artemis, who afflicted those who upset them. The Greeks thought that important figures such as Julius Caesar and Hercules had the disease.[1] The notable exception to this divine and spiritual view was that of the school of Hippocrates. In the fifth century BC, Hippocrates rejected the idea that the disease was caused by spirits. In his landmark work On the Sacred Disease, he proposed that epilepsy was not divine in origin and instead was a medically treatable problem originating in the brain.[1][104] He accused those of attributing a sacred cause to the disease of spreading ignorance through a belief in superstitious magic.[1] Hippocrates proposed that heredity was important as a cause, described worse outcomes if the disease presents at an early age, and made note of the physical characteristics as well as the social shame associated with it.[1] Instead of referring to it as the sacred disease, he used the term great disease, giving rise to the modern term grand mal, used for generalized seizures.[1] Despite his work detailing the physical origins of the disease, his view was not accepted at the time.[104] Evil spirits continued to be blamed until at least the 17th century.[104]

In most cultures, persons with epilepsy have been stigmatized, shunned, or even imprisoned; in the Salpêtrière, the birthplace of modern neurology, Jean-Martin Charcot found people with epilepsy side-by-side with the mentally ill, those with chronic syphilis, and the criminally insane.[108] In ancient Rome, epilepsy was known as the Morbus Comitialis ('disease of the assembly hall') and was seen as a curse from the gods. In northern Italy, epilepsy was once traditionally known as Saint Valentine's malady.[109]

In the mid-1800s the first effective anti-seizure medication, bromide, was introduced.[76] The first modern treatment, phenobarbital, was developed in 1912, with phenytoin coming into use in 1938.[110]

Society and culture[edit]

See also: List of people with epilepsy

Stigma[edit]

Stigma is commonly experienced, around the world, by those with epilepsy.[111] It can affect people economically, socially and culturally.[111] In India and China epilepsy may be used as justification to deny marriage.[3] People in some areas still believe those with epilepsy to be cursed.[10] In Tanzania, as in other parts of Africa, epilepsy is associated with possession by evil spirits, witchcraft, or poisoning and is believed by many to be contagious,[108] for which there is no evidence.[10] Before 1970 the United Kingdom had laws which prevented people with epilepsy from marrying.[3] The stigma may result in some people with epilepsy denying that they have ever had seizures.[36]

Economics[edit]

Seizures result in direct economic costs of about one billion dollars in the United States.[11] Epilepsy resulted in economic costs in Europe of around 15.5 billion Euros in 2004.[15] In India epilepsy is estimated to result in costs of 1.7 billion USD or 0.5% of the GDP.[3] It is the cause of about 1% of emergency department visits (2% for emergency departments for children) in the United States.[112]

Vehicles[edit]

See also: Epilepsy and driving

Those with epilepsy are at about twice the risk of being involved in a motor vehicular collision and thus in many areas of the world are not allowed to drive or only able to drive if certain conditions are met.[13] In some places physicians are required by law to report if a person has had a seizure to the licensing body while in others the requirement is only that they encourage the person in question to report it themselves.[13] Countries that require physician reporting include Sweden, Austria, Denmark and Spain.[13] Countries that require the individual to report include the UK, and New Zealand and the physician may report if they believe the individual has not already.[13] In Canada, the United States and Australia the requirements around reporting vary by province or state.[13] If seizures are well controlled most feel allowing driving is reasonable.[113] The amount of time a person must be free from seizures before they can drive varies by country.[113] Many countries require one to three years without seizures.[113] In the United States the time needed without a seizure is determined by each state and is between three months and one year.[113]

Those with epilepsy or seizures are typically denied a pilot license.[114] In Canada if an individual has had no more than one seizure, they may be considered after five years for a limited license if all other testing is normal.[115] Those with febrile seizures and drug related seizures may also be considered.[115] In the United States, the Federal Aviation Administration does not allow those with epilepsy to get a commercial pilot license.[116] Rarely, exceptions can be made for persons who have had an isolated seizure or febrile seizures and have remained free of seizures into adulthood without medication.[117] In the United Kingdom, a full national private pilot license requires the same standards as a professional driver's license.[118] This requires a period of ten years without seizures while off medications.[119] Those who do not meet this requirement may acquire a restricted license if free from seizures for five years.[118]

Support organizations[edit]

There are organizations that provide support for people and families affected by epilepsy. The Out of the Shadows campaign, a joint effort by the World Health Organization, the International League Against Epilepsy and the International Bureau for Epilepsy, provides help internationally.[3] The Joint Epilepsy Council serves the UK and Ireland.[46] In the United States, the Epilepsy Foundation is a national organization that works to increase the acceptance of those with the disease, their ability to function in society and to promote research for a cure.[120] The Epilepsy Foundation, some hospitals, and some individuals also run support groups in the United States.[121]

Research[edit]

Seizure prediction refers to attempts to forecast epileptic seizures based on the EEG before they occur.[122] As of 2011, no effective mechanism to predict seizures has been developed.[122] Kindling, where repeated exposures to events that could cause seizures eventually causes seizures more easily, has been used to create animal models of epilepsy.[123]

Gene therapy is being studied in some types of epilepsy.[124] Medications that alter immune function, such as intravenous immunoglobulins, are poorly supported by evidence.[125] Noninvasive stereotactic radiosurgery is, as of 2012, being compared to standard surgery for certain types of epilepsy.[126]

Other animals[edit]

Main article: Epilepsy in animals

Epilepsy occurs in a number of other animals including dogs and cats and is the most common brain disorder in dogs.[127] It is typically treated with anticonvulsants such as phenobarbital or bromide in dogs and phenobarbital in cats.[128] Imepitoin is also being used in dogs.[129] While generalized seizures in horses are fairly easy to diagnose, it may be more difficult in non-generalized seizures and EEGs may be useful.[130]
